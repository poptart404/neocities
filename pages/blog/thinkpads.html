The First Project: An IBM T43p "TrashPad"

I've had a growing fascination with the older 4:3 aspet ratio laptop screens, something I haven't seen since my very first laptop way back in the early/mid-2000's. Since I had a reasonable amount of parts to start assembling a T43p, that seemed like the place to start.


About the IBM T43p

Today the ThinkPad line is owned and manufactured by Lenovo. However, this line originated with IBM back in 1992, and wasn't sold to Lenovo until 2009. The T series has historically been (and appears to remain today) a line of moderate to high performance laptops marketed towards the business sector rather than consumers. As such they can be quite powerful and durable machines, with many components replaceable or upgradable for their enterprise customers.


The first line of T series laptops began with the T20 through the T23 in 2002. This was followed by the T30, then the T40 in 2003. The "p" suffix was first available on the T40p that year, which was the "performance" variant of the Thinkpad line. This pattern continued all the way up through 2005 with the launch of the T43 and T43p, which were the last models to be designed and manufactured by IBM. **add wikipedia citation**

In 2006 Lenovo released the T60 and T60p, the first Thinkpad models they designed and manufactured. From there came the T61 and T61p in 2007, and by 2008 the T400, the beginning of the well-known T4xx (and T5xx) series of Thinpads that Lenovo continued through 2019 and concluded with the T490, T495, and T590.


Starting Point: T43 and T43p Hardware from the Tech Dumps

Once I realized that I was holding (some) of the pieces of the last ThinkPad model made by IBM, I was determined to get this machine up and running again. So taking stock, here is what I had from my visit to the Tech Dumps in mid-February:


  • One T43 lid with screen. Screen shattered, bezel and cover battered but intact. Hinges intact. It felt like I got lucky here, that the hinges were left intact when this was separated from the body of the laptop.
  • One T43p body, both halves attached. Screen intact and appeared ok, left hinge broken, palmrest with touchpad and fingerprint reader present, lots of damage to the plastic on corners and edges. It appeared that someone had removed the keyboard and gone to town pulling components from the motherboard - no CPU, memory, fan, heatsink, card reader, hard drive, or disk drive to be found. Really just a motherboard, a screen, and a lot of beat up plastic.


As you may have guessed, this isn't actually much to go on. But despite the memory and chip shortages hardware of the 2005 vintage is still around and not so much in demand. Given that pretty much everything I encountered in the Tech Dumps was missing processors, memory, drives, etc., I went off to eBay to find my missing parts.


The first batch of items I ordered from eBay was as follows:

  • Intel Pentium M 770 Processor ($11.79)
  • Samsung 2GB (2x1GB) DDR2 RAM PC2-5300S 667MHz SODIMM Memory ($10.69)
  • IBM 16V 4.5A Power Supply ($14.98)
  • IBM ThinkPad Keyboard FRU: 08K4957/08K4986 ($32.08)
  • Dual Compact Flash CF to 44 Pin IDE/PATA 2.5" Adapter Enclosure ($21.39)
  • CMOS Battery ($3.84)


While waiting for my parts to arrive I started the process of cleaning and refurbishing the hardware I already had. First was to retrieve the good hinges from the shattered display panel and swap them in to the more complete T43p body. Luckily the T43p display was the one in better condition - this was particularly exciting because the "p" variant came with a 1440 x 1050 pixel display which is actually quite spacious. While swapping this in I also worked on cleaning the machine. A melamine foam sponge (think Magic Eraser) did a great job of restoring the top of the lid and minimizing the appearance of the scratches. Unfortunately much of this was also STICKY as heck, so I spent some quality time with a microfiber cloth and isopropyl alcohol to remove the sticky parts of the finish.


My History with Computers and How I Got Interested in Thinkpads

Early Days 1990s to early 2000s

I grew up learning how to build and maintain a computer in the 90's and early 00's, mainly old Dell and HP desktops out of old parts that my Dad was able to find on eBay or at computer shows. Things like basic BIOS setup and the Windows Control Panel in Windows 95, 98, and XP were familiar to me. When Dad was out of town for work and there was a problem with the family computer, I was usually the one who could diagnose the issue and get it running again.


Mid-2000s

In middle school I got my first laptop - a 5-ish year old business class laptop that an uncle had saved from his company's e-waste bin. I think it was a Dell, but I'm not 100% sure these days. It was probably a model from somewhere in the 1998 to 2002 range. The wireless card didn't work, and I think it only had a modem port, no ethernet. He put a fresh hard drive in it for me and off I went, armed with an ethernet PCMCIA card and later a USB wireless card. It ran best on Windows 2000 and couldn't handle XP, but was hilariously unstable even without my meddling in the OS, so I did a fresh re-install every 6 months or so. Because the driver for the USB wireless card had to be installed once the OS was up and running, I would end up spending an evening in the basement where the router sat up on a shelf, hanging out in a folding camp chair with an ethernet cable running from the laptop up to the router. It wasn't the most efficient, but it worked well enough and the computer was able to handle AIM and the occasional paper for school


Late 2000s and Dell Inspiron 1501

By high school I was able to save up enough between Christmas gifts and a part time job to buy my first new computer - a Dell Inspiron 1501 from the Dell Refurbished website a few days after Christmas. I think at the time it cost around $580 and even in hindsight it was worth every penny. I was particularly proud of myself at the time because Windows Vista had just come out, but becuase it was a refurbished model it just happened to have been shipped with Windows XP and had the "ready for Windows Vista" sticker on it. I very happily did not upgrade, having seen my neighbor's family computer already upgrading and having nothing but trouble.


I usually think of this as the first computer I really learned how to tinker with. I was running World of Warcraft (Burning Crusade of course) and having a blast finally having hardware that was up to date. After a few months (maybe a year?) I learned about Linux and attempted my first dual-boot - Ubuntu 08.04. I printed out the installation instructions on a few sheets of lined 3-hole-punch notebook paper I happened to have lying around so I had all the commands I would need and set to work. Of course I was hooked, and from that point on it was a rare point in time for me to not at least be dual-booting with Linux, more often than not it was my primary OS and Windows was just there in case I needed it to run a specific program.


In My T410 Era

Enter the Thinkpad - When I started college in 2010, my school had a laptop program. You purchased a laptop from the school that had a 4 year warranty and came with all the software you would need pre-installed. As an Engineering student this worked out to be quite worthwhile, especially because by then my little Dell Inspiron was starting to struggle with newer programs. So that August I got my first Thinkpad - a Lenovo T410. The original specs included a 500GB HDD, 4GB RAM, and a first-gen Intel i5 processor.


Little did I know with my shiny (? or matte I suppose) new T410 would be an even better gateway to developing my love of learning to configure my computer differently, explore a variety of operating systems, and exploring the world of FOSS software. If you're reading this wall of text, you're probably already aware that the thinkpad platform is and long has been well-supported across a multitude of Linux flavors and distributions.


I have undoubtedly lost count of the years of all of the different distributions and variations I installed on that little T410. I know that they included Fedora, Ubuntu, and my favorite at the time - Linux Mint. I was a big fan of Gnome 2 and disappointed when Gnome 3 came out, so I was very excited to switch over to Mint and the *Cinnamon* environment. While I generally maintained a second partition on my hard drive to run the occasional program for engineering classes in Windows 7, I completed most of my undergraduate work on Linux Mint and most of my typed assignments in LaTeX.


I was also very fortunate during this time to work part time at an IT Help Desk (if by some improbable chance one of y'all are reading this, you may remember us coming up with a "fraternity" that we dubbed "Eta Delta Floppy" and had printed on t-shirts around 2013 or so). During my three years there I had the absolute pleasure of learning from other engineering, computer science, and IT students more skills than I could possibly name. From a plethora of USB and CD utilities like UBCD and Hiren's to Arch and Puppy Linux, this was my first real exposure to a community of like-minded folks who loved to tinker away with computer hardware. I think I learned more from that job than in any class I took during that time and genuninely loved it. In the years since I've often remarked that given the chance today I would happily help out there for free.


Eventually my T410 suffered a motherboard failure sometime after graduation, and eventually I recycled it in favor of moving to a Macbook. Life took over and I put down my screwdriver and boot utilities for a few years. In hindsight I rather wish I hadn't, but such is life. I spent some time in the apple ecosystem. While this involved some nice shiny displays and pretty hardware, I've grown increasingly frustrated with the changes to the operating system and hardware.


Tech Dumps: Return of the Thinkpad

Now, another aspect of my time in college that I didn't take much advantage of at the time was access to the large e-waste bins in many of the buildings on campus. Also known as the Tech Dumps. These can contain anything from the shattered glass of a scanner bed (please do be careful in e-waste bins for stuff like that) to old server hardware or even desktop and laptop PCs. Some days the Tech Dumps giveth, and other days they just taketh a couple of hours of your afternoon.


I've recently been able to access a set of e-waste bins again, which have lovely signs above them asking folks to take only what they need and to return any un-used items so they may be properly recycled, rather than throwing them in the trash. A few weeks ago I made a rather impromptu visit, hoping to find one of those little blue hard drive clips for an older Dell Precision tower that houses my server. Not only did I find one, I was greeted with hardware the likes of which I hadn't seen since my Help Desk days.


As I arrived home that evening with the pieces of a T43 and T43p, a stripped down T22, a T61 in numerous pieces, and plenty of SATA cables (plus that elusive blue clip), I found my interest in older hardware rekindled. The older 7 row Thinkpad keyboard is second to none in my opinion, and even over a decade later since my T410 was my daily driver I still find myself searching for a little red TrackPoint nub. Not to mention the longer travel distance on the keys - I've been told that I type rather aggressively on today's thin keyboards - such comments as "what on earth did that keyboard do to you?" are not uncommon in my household. I came up with the idea of building a functional laptop using (where possible) only hardware recovered from these newfound Tech Dumps. Which brings us to today.
