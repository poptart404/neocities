The First Project: An IBM T43p "TrashPad"

I've had a growing fascination with the older 4:3 aspect ratio laptop screens, something I haven't seen since my very first laptop way back in the early/mid-2000's. Since I had a reasonable amount of parts to start assembling a T43p, that seemed like the place to start.


About the IBM T43p

IBM ThinkPad T43p


Photo Credit to ThinkWiki

Today the ThinkPad line is owned and manufactured by Lenovo. However, this line originated with IBM back in 1992, and wasn't sold to Lenovo until 2009. The T series has historically been (and appears to remain today) a line of moderate to high performance laptops marketed towards the business sector rather than consumers. As such they can be quite powerful and durable machines, with many components replaceable or upgradable for their enterprise customers.

The first line of T series laptops began with the T20 through the T23 in 2002. This was followed by the T30, then the T40 in 2003. The "p" suffix was first available on the T40p that year, which was the "performance" variant of the ThinkPad line. This pattern continued all the way up through 2005 with the launch of the T43 and T43p, which were the last models to be designed and manufactured by IBM. **add Wikipedia citation**

In 2006 Lenovo released the T60 and T60p, the first ThinkPad models they designed and manufactured. From there came the T61 and T61p in 2007, and by 2008 the T400, the beginning of the well-known T4xx (and T5xx) series of ThinkPads that Lenovo continued through 2019 and concluded with the T490, T495, and T590.


Starting Point: T43 and T43p Hardware from the Tech Dumps

For safety's sake, if you are ever looking through electronic waste of any sort please take appropriate steps to protect yourself by exercising caution and wearing PPE. Examples of PPE to consider include safety glasses, latex/vinyl gloves, thicker gloves to protect from sharp objects, a dust mask or respirator, long pants, long sleeves, and closed-toed shoes.

These bins are frequently full of hazardous materials and you can encounter things like lead, mercury, fine particulates (printer toner is a common one and quite nasty to inhale), and sharp objects (like broken glass). It is also wise to consider things like ensuring your tetanus shot is up to date before engaging in an activity like this. This is not a complete list of safety measures that can or should be taken, but is provided for context and as a reminder to be careful.

Personally I do all of the above and go with a friend - its more fun to share the experience, and if I do encounter something sharp or hazardous and need help I have a buddy. The buddy system - seriously underrated!!

In February 2026 I was poking through some local e-waste bins (affectionately known as the Tech Dumps) when I happened to find the top half of a ThinkPad T43. I had been mostly finding less interesting stuff in that particular bin, broken scanner beds and shredded VHS tapes. However, that top lid and screen (which was shattered and unusable) sparked a silly idea - what if I could find enough pieces to assemble and run an old IBM-era or even early Lenovo ThinkPad out of trashed parts? And thus the TrashPad idea was born.

The e-waste bins I occasionally visit are located in a series of buildings, so I continued along my way, toting my broken little display panel as inspiration. The next set that I visited had quite a bit of old server hardware, most of which wasn't useful to me. I did however find one of those little blue hard drive clips for an old Dell tower - which was perfect, I had been in need of one for my little server that I run on a Dell Precision tower. The drive in question had been sitting gently on top of a spare anti-static bag, so finding the correct clip was great news.

The last set of e-waste bins I visited were just what I was looking for. In them I found numerous old laptop and desktop components, ranging from Dell Optiplex towers dating to the late 2000's to a fairly recent model Chromebook. And much to my excitement, this was where I found most of the body of an IBM T43p, the same for a Lenovo T61, and in the best condition of the three, an IBM T22.
A couple of quick internet searches later and I realized I might have enough to start building my very own IBM T43p, the last ThinkPad model that was designed and manufactured by IBM.

So taking stock, here is what I had from my visit to the Tech Dumps in mid-February:

  • One T43 lid with screen. Screen shattered, bezel and cover battered but intact. Hinges intact. It felt like I got lucky here, that the hinges were left intact when this was separated from the body of the laptop.
  • One T43p body, both halves attached. Screen intact and appeared ok, left hinge broken, palm rest with touchpad and fingerprint reader present, lots of damage to the plastic on corners and edges. It appeared that someone had removed the keyboard and gone to town pulling components from the motherboard - no CPU, memory, fan, heatsink, card reader, hard drive, or disk drive to be found. Really just a motherboard, a screen, and a lot of beat up plastic.
As you may have guessed, this isn't actually much to go on. But despite the memory and chip shortages hardware of the 2005 vintage is still around and not so much in demand. Given that pretty much everything I encountered in the Tech Dumps was missing processors, memory, drives, etc., I went off to eBay to find my missing parts.


The first batch of items I ordered from eBay was as follows:

  • Intel Pentium M 770 Processor ($11.79)
  • Samsung 2GB (2x1GB) DDR2 RAM PC2-5300S 667MHz SODIMM Memory ($10.69)
  • IBM 16V 4.5A Power Supply ($14.98)
  • IBM ThinkPad Keyboard FRU: 08K4957/08K4986 ($32.08)
  • Dual Compact Flash CF to 44 Pin IDE/PATA 2.5" Adapter Enclosure ($21.39)
  • CMOS Battery ($3.84)

While waiting for my parts to arrive I started the process of cleaning and refurbishing the hardware I already had. First was to retrieve the good hinges from the shattered display panel and swap them in to the more complete T43p body. Luckily the T43p display was the one in better condition - this was particularly exciting because the "p" variant came with a 1440 x 1050 pixel display which is actually quite spacious. While swapping this in I also worked on cleaning the machine. A melamine foam sponge (think Magic Eraser) did a great job of restoring the top of the lid and minimizing the appearance of the scratches. Unfortunately much of this was also STICKY as heck, so I spent some quality time with a microfiber cloth and isopropyl alcohol to remove the sticky parts of the finish.
